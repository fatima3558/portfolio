Projects

  • Connect4!

    Lesson 1: Speak your interlocutor's language.

    Patterns are patterns, and patterns usually have a mathematical basis for their pattern-y-ness. All you have to do is find the code that explains it in the most accurate and, hopefully, most elegant way possible.

    While I was building my Connect4 app, I was having a difficult time writing logic for my app to understand what constitutes '4-in-a-row', or a win. Translating the idea of a 2-dimensional game board with different colors was proving very difficult - until I realized that there isn't any need for me to be bound to the ways in which humans usually conceptualize the game board.

    Here's what I mean: to humans, the game board looks like a 6x7 grid with 2 different colors in play. When four colors are in a consecutive horizontal, vertical, or diagonal line, it's easy to see visually. But what if we didn't see a grid, but we imagined the board as a one-dimensional line of squares? And what if we said, instead of a 'horizontal win', we can identify that win by checking if there are four squares of the same color in this line that are the same distance away from each other? Now all we need is math - no eyes, no need to imagine 'space', and thankfully, no need to dive into those kinds of conversations with a computer.

  • LitShare

    Lesson 3: Be willing to question things you thought you knew.

    For this project, I worked in a team of three and we split both apps up into routes that each of us was in charge of. I was able to lead the conversation in my team about the big picture of what our apps should do and in what order. These are conversations I feel strong in. But perhaps more importantly, for this project, I was able to tackle something I had much less of an understanding of: authorization and authentication.

    This part of the project left me mentally breathless and wishing for more time to puzzle over Python and Flask. Having written code in JavaScript for the past two months, I found many of the ways in which apps work in JavaScript/Express don't translate directly into Python/Flask. It might be something simple: sometimes it felt like nearly everything I needed to do in Python outside of pure data manipulation required downloading an additional module. Or it can be more complex, like figuring out exactly how to make the app do certain things only if the user is logged in.

    Ultimately, once I took a moment to open myself up to the possibility that any Python solution for a problem may not be even remotely like a JavaScript solution, I found myself much more able to appreciate Python and Flask for what they are.
